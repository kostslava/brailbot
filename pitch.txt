BRAILBOX — 5-Minute Demo Pitch
================================

[~30 sec] HOOK
--------------
"1 in 4 visually impaired adults say the hardest part isn't reading —
it's being left out of the story. BrailBox fixes that."

Hold up the device. Let them see it.

"This is a community storytelling kiosk that anyone can walk up to
and experience a story in three ways at once: they hear it, they see
it on screen, and they feel it — in real-time braille."


[~45 sec] THE PROBLEM
---------------------
- Braille literacy is declining. Only 10% of blind Americans read braille,
  down from over 50% in the 1960s. The hardware is expensive and the
  content is limited.
- Most assistive tech treats accessibility as an afterthought — a screen
  reader bolted on top of something built for sighted users.
- There's no simple, community-driven way to share stories that are
  accessible by default.


[~60 sec] THE SOLUTION
----------------------
BrailBox has two sides:

1. THE WEB APP — anyone, anywhere, submits a short story. AI moderates
   it for safety, classifies it by theme and mood, cleans up the text,
   and publishes it to our database. No login, no friction.

2. THE KIOSK — a Raspberry Pi in a library, school, or community center.
   You walk up, press a key, and say what kind of story you want.
   "Tell me something motivational." It finds a match, reads the summary,
   and asks if you're interested. Say yes, and the full story plays —
   spoken aloud, displayed on screen, and rendered letter-by-letter on
   a physical braille display built from three hobby servos and a
   3D-printed mechanism.

   The braille and audio are synchronized sentence by sentence so nothing
   gets out of sync. After the story, you rate it with your voice — good
   or bad — and that feeds back into the ranking so the best stories
   rise to the top.


[~90 sec] LIVE DEMO
--------------------
Do this live. Practice it twice beforehand.

1. Open the submit page on your phone/laptop. Type or paste a short
   story (have one ready — 3-4 sentences, motivational theme).
   Hit submit. Show it going through moderation + classification.
   "Published."

2. Walk over to the kiosk (Pi + laptop + braille display).
   Press a key. Wait for "What story would you want to hear?"
   Say: "I want a motivational story."
   It finds the story you just submitted. Summary plays.
   "Is that good?" — "Yes."
   Full story plays. Point out the three outputs happening together:
     - Audio from the speaker
     - Text on screen, sentence by sentence
     - Servos physically rotating to form each braille letter

3. After the story: "Was that good?" — "Good."
   Show the rating increment on the sidebar.

Keep narrating what's happening as it runs. Judges shouldn't have to
guess what they're looking at.


[~30 sec] HOW WE BUILT IT
--------------------------
- Next.js web app + Supabase database + Backboard AI for moderation
- Raspberry Pi 4 running a Python Flask server
- OpenAI Whisper for on-device speech recognition (no cloud STT needed)
- pyttsx3 for text-to-speech
- 3 MG90S servos controlled via GPIO + pigpio for jitter-free braille
- 3D-printed servo attachments with 1-dot and 2-dot bump patterns
- Real-time sync over WebSockets between audio, screen, and servos

Total hardware cost for the braille display: under $15.


[~30 sec] WHAT'S NEXT
----------------------
- Multi-language support (braille is universal, the encoding changes)
- A full 6-servo, 2-character display for faster reading
- Partner with libraries and schools for pilot deployments
- Let users contribute stories by voice, not just text


[~15 sec] CLOSE
---------------
"A $15 braille display. Community stories. Three senses at once.
BrailBox makes storytelling accessible — not as an afterthought,
but as the whole point."


================================
JUDGE Q&A — LIKELY QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS
================================

Q: Why servos instead of solenoids or a commercial braille display?
A: Commercial refreshable braille displays cost $3,000-$6,000. Ours
   costs $15 in servos and filament. Servos are slow for continuous
   reading, but for a kiosk experience synced with audio, speed isn't
   the bottleneck — the narration pace is.

Q: How does the AI moderation work?
A: Stories go through a Backboard-hosted LLM that acts as a safety
   filter. It's intentionally lenient — approves humor, satire, and
   casual language, but catches hate speech, threats, and explicit
   content. A second LLM pass classifies theme, mood, age group, and
   generates a clean version of the text.

Q: Can this scale?
A: The web side is serverless Next.js + Supabase — scales by default.
   Each kiosk is a self-contained Pi that just needs WiFi. Deploy a
   new one by flashing an SD card and plugging in.

Q: What about braille grade 2 (contractions)?
A: We're using grade 1 (letter-by-letter) right now for simplicity
   and because it maps cleanly to audio timing. Grade 2 is a clear
   next step.

Q: Is Whisper accurate enough on a Pi?
A: With the "base" model and a decent USB mic, yes — especially for
   short phrases like "motivational story" or "yes." We keep the
   expected vocabulary narrow, which helps a lot.
